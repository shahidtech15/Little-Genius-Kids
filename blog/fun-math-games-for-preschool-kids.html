Fun Math Games for Preschool Kids

Sarah Johnson, Early Childhood Educator March 17, 2026 6 min read
Back to Blog

Here's a truth that every early childhood educator knows: young children don't learn maths by sitting still and memorising. They learn it by touching, moving, counting, laughing — by doing. The best maths lesson for a preschooler is one that feels like a game, not a lesson at all.

The good news? You don't need expensive materials or a teaching degree to play great maths games with your child. Everything you need is already at home. Here are four brilliantly simple maths games — and the skills they secretly build.

🧠 Why Games Beat Worksheets for Under-5s

Research in early childhood education consistently shows that play-based learning produces stronger, longer-lasting mathematical understanding in children under 6 than formal instruction. When learning feels like play, children engage more deeply, repeat activities voluntarily, and connect concepts to real life — exactly what maths needs.

📚 Why Maths Games Matter for Preschoolers

Long before children can write a number, they develop a natural "number sense" — an intuitive feel for quantities, patterns, and relationships. Maths games nurture this sense in concrete, meaningful ways:

🏗️

Foundational Skills

Counting, one-to-one correspondence, and number recognition are the bedrock of all future mathematics. Games build these skills naturally through repetition that feels like fun, not drilling.

🔍

Critical Thinking

Even simple games require children to make decisions, spot patterns, and solve small problems — building the analytical thinking that powers all learning, not just maths.

🌟

Curiosity & Confidence

When maths is fun, children ask questions, take risks, and try again after mistakes. This positive relationship with numbers — formed early — shapes a child's confidence in maths for years to come.

🎮 4 Fun Maths Game Ideas to Try Today

1. Counting Objects 🧸

The simplest game is the most powerful. Gather a handful of any objects — toy cars, pasta pieces, blocks, grapes — and count them together. The magic is in making counting physical: touch each item as you say its number.

  • Beginner: Count up to 5 using fingers or small objects
  • Intermediate: Ask "How many?" after arranging objects in a line
  • Advanced: Sort by colour or size first, then count each group
  • The secret skill being built: One-to-one correspondence — the understanding that each object gets exactly one number

2. Number Hunt 🔍

Write numbers 1–10 on pieces of paper or sticky notes and hide them around a room. Send your child on a hunt to find them all — then arrange them in order when found.

  • Add a twist: ask your child to find a specific number first: "Can you find number 4?"
  • Upgrade: hide two copies of each number and play a matching version
  • Outdoor version: write numbers in chalk on a path and ask your child to hop to the number you call
  • The secret skill: Number recognition, sequencing, and the concept that numbers have a fixed order

3. Shape Sorting Games 🔷

Combine geometry and counting in one activity. Gather household objects of different shapes — round lids, square boxes, triangular snack bags — and sort them into groups.

  • Count how many objects are in each shape group
  • Ask: "Which group has more?" — an early introduction to comparison and greater than/less than
  • Make outlines of each shape on paper and match objects to their silhouette
  • The secret skill: Classification, spatial reasoning, and number comparison — three higher-order maths skills hiding inside a shape game

4. Dice Games 🎲

A single dice is one of the most versatile maths tools you own. Roll it, count the dots, and build from there.

  • Dot counting: Roll and count the dots — then collect that many small objects
  • Race to 10: Both players roll; whoever reaches exactly 10 objects first wins
  • Add it up: Roll two dice and count the total dots — a natural introduction to addition
  • Story dice: Roll and make up a story using that number: "The bear found 5 apples…"
  • The secret skill: Subitising (instantly recognising quantities without counting), addition, and number sense
💡 No Dice? No Problem!

Make your own dice from a small cardboard box — draw dots on each face with a marker. Or use a spinner made from a pencil and a paper clip. The homemade version is often more exciting than the real thing.

👨‍👩‍👧 Tips for Parents: Making Maths Fun Every Day

  • Keep sessions short and joyful — 5 to 10 minutes of engaged play beats 30 minutes of reluctant seatwork every time. Stop while it's still fun so your child asks to play again tomorrow.
  • Celebrate the process, not just the answer — "I love how you counted those carefully!" builds more confidence than "Well done, you got it right!" Effort and strategy deserve as much praise as correct answers.
  • Weave maths into daily routines — "Can you put 3 crackers on your plate?" "Let's count the stairs!" "Which pile of socks has more?" Daily maths is the most powerful maths because it shows children that numbers are genuinely useful.
  • Let your child take the lead — if they want to change the rules mid-game, go with it. Inventing rules is creative maths thinking at its finest.
  • Play alongside — don't test — the moment a game feels like a test, children disengage. Stay curious yourself: "I wonder how many there are? Let's count together…"

Conclusion 🌟

Maths doesn't start in a classroom with worksheets and pencils. It starts on the living room floor with a handful of blocks, a laughing child, and a parent who says "let's count these together." Every counting game, every dice roll, every shape sorted is a brick in the foundation of mathematical thinking your child will build on for their entire life.

Start today. Start small. Make it joyful. That's the whole secret to raising a child who genuinely loves maths.

The Maths Game Formula 🎲

Counting Objects + Number Hunt + Shape Sorting + Dice Games = a preschooler who sees numbers as friends, not foes. Keep sessions short, keep them fun, and weave counting into everything.
