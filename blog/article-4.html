10 Fun Learning Techniques That Actually Work for Preschoolers

Sarah Johnson, Early Childhood Educator March 14, 2026 8 min read
Back to Blog

Every parent wants to support their young child's learning. But here's the challenge: traditional teaching methods — sitting still, listening quietly, memorizing facts — are fundamentally unsuited for how preschool-age brains actually work.

Young children don't learn by being taught. They learn by doing, playing, exploring, and connecting new ideas to things they already love. The good news? The techniques that work best are also the most fun — for children and adults alike.

Here are 10 proven, practical approaches that transform learning from a chore into a genuine adventure.

1. Game-Based Learning

When children play games, they practice skills they don't even realize they're developing. A simple matching game builds memory and concentration. A sorting activity develops logical thinking and classification skills. A board game with dice teaches number recognition and counting without a single worksheet.

The key feature of game-based learning is that children are intrinsically motivated — they want to play, so they'll gladly practice a skill dozens of times to win or improve. That repetition is exactly what builds mastery.

2. Storytelling and Narrative Learning

The human brain is wired for story. When information is embedded in a narrative — with characters, conflict, and resolution — it's far more memorable than isolated facts. This is why fairy tales teach moral lessons, and why "Jack and the Beanstalk" is remembered 100 years later while flash-card facts are forgotten by lunchtime.

Use stories to introduce any concept:

  • "The number 5 went looking for her friend, the number 2, because together they made 7..." (addition concept)
  • "Mr. Circle was rolling down the hill when he met Ms. Triangle, who had pointy corners..." (shapes concept)
  • Read books and then ask: "What do YOU think happens next?"

3. Music and Movement Integration

Music accesses parts of the brain that straight instruction does not. When children sing information, it travels through auditory processing, rhythm comprehension, and memory centers simultaneously — making it dramatically more memorable.

But music's learning power multiplies when combined with movement. When a child sings "Head, Shoulders, Knees and Toes" while touching each body part, they're integrating auditory, visual, kinesthetic, and linguistic learning pathways all at once.

  • Make up simple songs for any lesson: colors, number sequences, days of the week
  • Use clapping rhythms to practice syllable breakdown ("ba-na-na")
  • Dance to songs about what you're learning
  • Use our Rhymes and Songs section for ready-made learning through music

4. The "I Do, We Do, You Do" Method

This simple three-stage approach is used by skilled teachers everywhere, and it works beautifully with preschoolers:

  1. I Do: You demonstrate the skill completely. "Watch me sort these buttons by color. Blue ones here, red ones there..."
  2. We Do: You do it together. "Now let's sort together. You hold this blue one — where does it go?"
  3. You Do: The child does it independently. "Now you try! Sort all the rest yourself."

This scaffolded approach builds confidence because children experience success at each stage before moving forward. They never feel thrown in the deep end, and they feel the satisfaction of eventual independence.

5. Connecting Learning to Real Life

Abstract concepts are difficult for preschoolers. Concrete, real-world applications make everything click. When children see the immediate, practical use of what they're learning, engagement skyrockets.

  • Counting: Count apples at the grocery store, stairs at home, socks in the laundry
  • Colors: Name colors of traffic lights, flowers, food on the plate
  • Letters: Point out first letters of family members' names on signs and food packaging
  • Shapes: Find circles, squares, and triangles in the architecture around you

6. Repetition — But Make It Interesting

Repetition is essential for early learning — but mindless repetition dulls engagement. The secret is to repeat the same concept through different activities, approaches, and contexts. Learning the letter "A" through:

  • Singing the ABC song (auditory)
  • Tracing "A" in sand (kinesthetic)
  • Spotting "A" on a cereal box (visual)
  • Eating apples while saying "/a/ /a/ apple!" (experiential)

Each exposure through a different channel strengthens the neural network. Children don't experience this as repetition — they experience it as discovery.

7. Positive Reinforcement That Motivates

Specific, sincere praise is one of the most powerful learning tools available. Not generic "Good job!" praise — but specific acknowledgment: "I noticed how you kept trying even when the puzzle was hard. That's what makes you a great learner."

Star charts, sticker rewards, and celebration dances all harness the brain's reward system to create positive associations with learning. Our interactive games award stars for correct answers — and children light up every single time.

8. Child-Led Learning: Follow Their Curiosity

When a child shows fascination with something — dinosaurs, butterflies, construction vehicles — lean in rather than redirecting. Child-led inquiry is some of the deepest learning there is:

  • Check out library books on their favorite topic
  • Count dinosaur species (math), spell their names (literacy), draw their shapes (art)
  • Turn obsessions into projects — the engagement carries the learning

9. Outdoor Learning and Nature Play

The natural world is a curriculum that needs no planning. Outside, children naturally practice observation, classification, problem-solving, physical challenges, and sensory exploration. Research consistently shows that outdoor free play reduces stress, improves attention, and supports emotional regulation.

  • Nature scavenger hunts (find something red, something rough, something alive)
  • Leaf and bark rubbings (textural art)
  • Muddy puddle science ("What happens when we add water to dirt?")
  • Cloud watching and naming shapes ("That one looks like a rabbit!")

10. Asking Great Questions

Great teachers don't always give answers — they ask great questions. Open-ended questions that have no single right answer are the richest for young learners:

  • "What do you notice about these two things?"
  • "What happens if we add more water?"
  • "How did you figure that out?"
  • "What do you think she's feeling?"
  • "If you could change the ending, what would happen?"

These questions invite genuine thinking rather than recall. They build critical thinking, language skills, and the confidence to share ideas. And they communicate something essential: that what your child thinks matters.

The Master Principle 🌟

The best learning technique is whichever one your specific child engages with joyfully and consistently. Every child is different. Experiment freely, follow their lead, and make fun your north star. A child who loves learning will always find a way to learn.
